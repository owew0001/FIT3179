An Exploration of Australian Endangered Plant Species

Where are all of these 'at risk' plants

The following map visualises the distribution of Endangered Australian plant species from the results of a 2024 survey completed by the Threatened Species Index(TSX) (1). TSX aimed to track the presence of endangered Australian plant species across the states. Along the coast of New South Wales, Victoria and parts of South Australia and Western Australia, there are large concentrations of threatened species. This is often believed to be due to the high rates of urbanisation leading to the destruction of native habitats of local flora (2).

This bar chart displays the results of the individual records of the TSX survey, New South Wales contains the largest number of endangered species collected within the survey with 69 different species being at an EPBC status of 'Near Threatened' or above, whereas the Northern Territory contains the least endangered species with 1 endangered species recorded. Out of the 8 Australian states, only half of them make up 92.7% of the endangered species collected in this survey. It must be noted, this survey was only able to collect samples on 206 out of the 1,451 species that are currently considered "at Risk" in Australia and as such can only give us a limited insight into the distribution of 'At Risk' species throughout Australia.

How bad is it?

The following area chart displays time series data from the Australian Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water and shows the increase of 'At Risk' species within Australia from 2000-2025.

It appears that as times goes on, the number of 'at risk' species increases at a faster and faster rate. This is apparant from the rapid widening of the 'Critically Endangered' section of the graph as we approach 2025.

Best estimates place the number of plant species in Australia at around 18,200 different species (4), meaning that at the current moment, approximately 8% of the plant species within Australia are at risk of endangerment and subsequent extinction.

What is causing this?

The underlying cause of plant endangerment is the destruction of natural habitats from deforestation.

Utilsing data from the Global Forest Watch (5) we can get an idea of where deforestation is occuring the most within Australia. We see that New South Wales, Victoria and Western Australia all have high rates of deforestation which is consistent with the map displaying endangered plant species shown above.

We see a notable uptick in 2020 indicating that an extreme event likely occurred at this time.

What does deforestation look like across Australia?

Utilising spatial data from Global Forest Watch (5) we can take a closer look at the distribution of deforestation across Australia. We can see that along the coasts is where we see the highest concentrations of deforestation, likely due to the concentration of human infrastructure and expansion into natural habitats. Comparing data from 2005 to 2023 , we can see that there is a definite increase in the acreage of forests being lost again with much of the red being concentrated near human civilisations.

What is the root cause of deforestation?

As with any longstanding problem, we see that deforestation is a multifaceted problem with many causes. However, it is clear that the largest contributer to deforestation within Australia is Wildfires. This is due to Australia's dry and arid climate, naturally flammable vegetation, and human interference (6). Logging is the second largest contributer with it consistently causing >18% of the deforestation within Australia. Notably this was not the trend in 2019 due to the global COVID pandemic and numerous intense wildfires in the 'Black Summer' period lasting from June 2019 - March 2020 (7).

Wildfires in Australia

Wildfires appeared to be by far the largest contibuter to deforestation by far. Lets take a closer look at Wildfires. Presented below is Fire Alert data from the VIIRS satellite from NASA (8). We can clearly see that the largest number of fire alerts appear within the October - March period, this is due to bushfire season within Victoria and New South Wales (7). Notably 2012, 2019,2020 and 2023 had a much higher number of fire alerts than the average however this is due to the intense wildfires that occurred during these years.
